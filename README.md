# iisauh wallet

**iisauh wallet** is a personal finance dashboard that helps you track your money in one place. You enter your numbers yourself—no automatic bank connections—and the app gives you a clear picture of where you stand.

The app helps you track:

- **Cash balances** — Bank accounts and cash on hand
- **Credit card balances** — What you owe on each card
- **Pending inbound and outbound money** — Transfers and payments that haven’t settled yet
- **Recurring expenses and income** — Rent, paychecks, subscriptions, and other repeating items
- **Spending breakdowns** — Where your money goes, by category (with charts)
- **Public loans** — Federal student loans (one simplified card)
- **Private loans** — Private student loans (each loan tracked separately)
- **Loan payments and payoff timelines** — How much you pay now and when loans could be paid off
- **Investing balances** — Investment account totals you enter manually

The goal is to give you **a clear financial picture in one place**: cash, credit, pending items, loans, spending, and recurring flow—all under your control.

---

# Who This App Is For

This app is useful if you:

- **Track monthly expenses** — You want to see where your money goes and keep spending by category.
- **Manage student loans** — You have federal and/or private loans and want to see Payment(now), future payments, and payoff estimates in one dashboard.
- **Understand cash flow** — You want to see recurring income and expenses, upcoming bills, and how pending transfers affect your snapshot.
- **Want a full financial snapshot** — You like having cash, credit cards, pending items, loans, and spending all in one app without linking bank accounts.
- **Prefer manual control** — You’re comfortable entering your own numbers and don’t want an app that automatically pulls from your accounts.

It’s designed for everyday users who want clarity and control, not for automatic syncing with banks.

# Quick Start for a Brand New User

Follow this order when you open the app for the first time:

1. **Open the app** in your browser (phone or computer).
2. **Go to Settings** if you want to adjust appearance (theme, accent color, font) before you start. This is optional.
3. **Add your accounts and balances** — In the **Snapshot** tab, add your bank accounts and credit cards and enter their current balances.
4. **Add recurring expenses and recurring income** — In the **Recurring** tab, add things that repeat (e.g. monthly salary, rent, subscriptions). This helps the app show expected cash flow and supports loan-related features.
5. **Add your loans** — In the **Loans** tab, add any public (federal) and/or private student loans. Enter balances, interest rates, and payment details so the app can show Payment(now) and payoff estimates.
6. **Review Snapshot** — Check the Snapshot tab to see your overall picture: cash, credit, pending items, and final net cash.
7. **Review Spending** — Add a few purchases if you like, or set up categories in Settings first. Then use Spending to see where money goes.
8. **Export a JSON backup** — In Settings, use **Export JSON** to save a copy of your data to your device. Do this once setup is done so you don’t lose your data if the browser is cleared.

After that, you can use the app daily: log spending, move items to upcoming or pending, recompute loan payments when things change, and export backups regularly.

# Main Tabs Explained

The app is organized into tabs (usually at the bottom on mobile). Each tab has a specific job.

---

## Snapshot

**What it shows:**  
Snapshot is your main financial overview. It shows:

- **Cash** — Total in your bank accounts (and any cash you track).
- **Credit card balances** — What you owe on each card and in total.
- **Pending inbound** — Money you’re expecting to receive (e.g. a transfer that hasn’t hit yet).
- **Pending outbound** — Money that’s “on its way out” (e.g. a payment or transfer you’ve initiated but that hasn’t cleared).
- **Final net cash** — One number that combines cash, minus credit card debt, plus/minus pending items. It answers: “If everything settled right now, where would I stand?”

**Why it matters:**  
Snapshot is the only place that combines cash, credit, and pending in one view. It helps you see your real position at a glance and avoid thinking you have more (or less) than you do.

**What the numbers mean:**  
- Green numbers usually mean money in your favor (cash, pending inbound).  
- Red usually means money owed (credit card debt, pending outbound).  
- Final net cash is the bottom line after everything is accounted for.

---

## Spending

**What it shows:**  
Spending shows where your money goes. You log each purchase or expense (amount, category, optional notes). The app then shows:

- A **spending distribution** — Often as a pie chart or list by category (e.g. food, rent, fun).
- A **total for the period** — You can filter by “This month,” “Last month,” “All time,” or a custom date range.
- Optionally, a breakdown by payment method (e.g. by card) instead of by category.

**Why it matters:**  
Spending helps you see patterns: how much you spend on food, subscriptions, etc. The pie chart and category list make it easy to spot where most of your money goes.

**What it helps you understand:**  
You can see if you’re over budget in certain categories, track progress toward spending goals (e.g. in SUB Tracker), and keep a history of what you bought and when.

---

## Upcoming

**What it shows:**  
Upcoming shows money you expect to receive or pay in the future. You add items like “Next paycheck” or “Rent due March 1” with amounts and dates.

**What upcoming items are:**  
They are planned income or expenses that haven’t happened yet. They’re not in your bank balance yet—they’re on the calendar so you can plan.

**How pending inbound/outbound fits:**  
When the time comes, you can **move** an upcoming item to **Pending outbound** (e.g. “I just sent the rent”) or **Pending inbound** (e.g. “I just initiated a deposit”). That keeps your Snapshot accurate: the money is no longer “upcoming,” it’s “in motion.” When the transfer actually clears, you can post or adjust balances in Snapshot.

**How it helps you plan:**  
You see what’s due when, so you can avoid surprises and plan around big payments or income.

---

## Loans

**What it shows:**  
Loans is where you track public (federal) and private student loans. It shows:

- **Public loans** — One card where you enter an estimated monthly payment and optionally total balance and average rate.
- **Private loans** — Each loan on its own card with balance, rate, payment amount or term, and optional “ranges” (e.g. deferred, interest-only, full repayment).
- **Payment(now)** — The total monthly loan payment the app treats as “current” for planning.
- **After-grace / future payment** — Estimates of what you’ll pay after grace or in the future, and payoff timelines.

**Public vs private:**  
- **Public** = federal student loans. The app doesn’t fetch them automatically; you enter an estimated payment and optional balance/rate.  
- **Private** = private student (or other) loans. You add each one with balance, rate, and payment details. The app calculates payments and payoff from what you enter.

**Payment tracking and payoff timeline:**  
The app uses your balances, rates, and payment ranges to compute how much you pay now and when each loan could be paid off. You can recompute when balances or rates change, and use refinance and consolidation as **simulations only** (no real changes to your loans).

**Refinance and consolidation tools:**  
- **Refinance** (on a private loan card) — Simulates one loan with a new rate and term; shows the new payment.  
- **See consolidated loan** (in the Private section) — Simulates combining all private loans into one and shows the new payment compared to your current total private payment.  
Both are for “what if?” only; they don’t change your real loan data.

# Detailed Loan Explanation

The Loans tab is one of the most detailed parts of the app. Here’s how each piece works in plain English.

---

## Public Loans

**What the public section is for:**  
It’s for federal (government) student loans. The app doesn’t connect to the government; you enter what you know.

**Estimated monthly public loan payment:**  
This is the amount you enter as your best estimate of what you pay (or will pay) each month on federal loans. The app uses it for the main **Payment(now)** total when you add it via “Add to Payment(now)” or “Use as current payment.”

**Total public balance / average rate:**  
Optional. If you enter a total balance and average interest rate, the app can use them for dashboard totals (e.g. total debt). They don’t change how your real federal loans work—they’re for your own tracking.

**How “Add to Payment(now)” works:**  
When you click **Add to Payment(now)** in the Public section, the app takes your entered estimated public payment and **adds it** to the main **Payment(now)** number shown at the top of the Loans tab. So Payment(now) then includes your public loan payment. It doesn’t change your loan balance; it only updates the single “current payment” number the app displays.

**What “Use as current payment” means:**  
**Use as current payment** tells the app to treat the amount you entered as the **current** public loan payment and feed it into Payment(now). So from then on, that amount is part of the visible Payment(now) until you change it or edit Payment(now) manually.

**What “Use first payment date” means:**  
**Use first payment date** lets you set the date when your first federal payment is due. The app can use that for planning. If the app supports it, it can also automatically add the public payment to Payment(now) when that date is reached. You can turn that auto-add on or off (e.g. “Active” or “Paused”).

---

## Private Loans

**What private loans are:**  
Private loans are non-federal loans (e.g. from a bank or lender). You add each one separately with balance, interest rate, and payment details.

**What ranges mean:**  
For each private loan you can set **ranges**—periods of time when the loan is in a certain “mode.” The app uses the range that’s active **today** to decide how much that loan contributes to Payment(now) and to future estimates.

**Deferred:**  
During a **deferred** (or forbearance) range, you’re usually not making full payments. The app may treat the payment as zero or a special value for that period. Interest might still accrue depending on the loan type.

**Interest-only:**  
During an **interest-only** range, you pay only the interest each month. The principal (the amount you owe) doesn’t go down (or goes down very slowly). The app calculates the interest-only payment and uses it for Payment(now) and estimates.

**Full repayment:**  
During **full repayment**, you pay interest plus principal so the balance goes down over time. The app calculates a standard amortized payment based on balance, rate, and term and uses it for Payment(now) and payoff estimates.

**Custom monthly payment:**  
**Custom monthly** lets you enter a fixed amount you pay each month. The app uses that amount instead of calculating one. Useful when your actual payment doesn’t match a standard formula.

---

## Payment(now) Explained

**What it means:**  
**Payment(now)** is the total monthly loan payment the app shows as “current.” It’s the number you see in the Loans summary (and sometimes elsewhere). It can include both public and private loan payments, depending on what you’ve added or recomputed.

**How it is used:**  
- It’s the “current loan payment” for your dashboard.  
- Recurring items can be set to **use the current loan payment** from the Loans tab—so a recurring “student loan payment” expense can automatically use Payment(now).  
- When you **edit Payment(now)**, you override this number manually (e.g. set it to 0 or a specific amount). That override is for display and planning only; it doesn’t change your real loan balances.

**How it connects to recurring items:**  
In the Recurring tab, you can choose an amount source like “current visible Payment(now) from Loans tab.” So when Payment(now) goes up or down (because you recomputed or added loan payments), that recurring expense can follow it. That keeps your planned expenses in line with what the Loans tab shows.

**How it can be edited:**  
You can **edit Payment(now)** by clicking the “Payment (now)” label in the Loans summary (or via an edit control, depending on the app). A small window or form lets you type a new value (e.g. 0 or 500). That value **replaces** the displayed Payment(now). It does **not** change your loan balances or the underlying math—it’s a display override. Useful when you want to plan with a specific number or zero out loans for a view.

---

## After-Grace / Future Payment

**What it means:**  
**After-grace** (or “future payment”) is the app’s estimate of what you’ll pay **after** a grace period or forbearance ends—when you’re in full repayment. For private loans, the app uses your balance, rate, and term (or payment) to estimate that amount.

**Where it appears:**  
It often appears in the Loans tab in a breakdown or “future estimated payments” section. You may see “total private after-grace” or similar—that’s the sum of what each private loan would require in full repayment.

**Why it matters:**  
It helps you plan: “Once I’m out of grace, what will I owe each month?” The **consolidation simulator** compares a hypothetical consolidated payment to this after-grace total so you can see if consolidating would raise or lower your payment.

---

## Recompute Explained

**What it does:**  
**Recompute your Payment(now)** recalculates private loan payments from:

- Current balances  
- Interest rates  
- The **active** payment range for each loan (deferred, interest-only, full repayment, or custom)

After recalculating, the app updates the total **Payment(now)**. Depending on how the app is set up, it may also apply one “recompute cycle” to balances (e.g. interest accrual or a payment step). The exact behavior is in the app; the important part is that **recompute brings Payment(now) in line with your current loan data**.

**Why someone would use it:**  
Use recompute when something has changed: you made a payment (and posted it), you updated a balance or rate, or you changed a range. Recompute makes the displayed Payment(now) match the new situation.

**How it connects to balances:**  
Recompute uses the **current** balance and rate for each loan. If you’ve posted a payment, the balance is lower, so the next recompute will use that lower balance and may show a lower Payment(now) (or a different payoff timeline). So: posting updates balances → recompute uses those updated balances → Payment(now) and estimates stay accurate.

---

## Refinance Explained

**What it simulates:**  
**Refinance** (on a private loan card) simulates taking **that one loan** and refinancing it: you enter a new interest rate and/or term (length of the loan). The app then shows what the **new monthly payment** would be.

**How it uses the after-grace value:**  
The refinance simulator often shows the new payment and may compare it to the current or after-grace payment for that loan so you can see if refinancing would increase or decrease your monthly payment. It does **not** change your real loan—it’s a “what if?” tool.

---

## Consolidation Simulator Explained

**What it simulates:**  
**See consolidated loan** (in the Private loans section) simulates combining **all** your private loans into one. You enter a new interest rate and term (in years). The app uses your **total private loan balance** and shows:

- What one consolidated monthly payment would be  
- How that compares to your **current total private after-grace** payment (the amount you’d pay if you kept all loans separate and entered full repayment)

**How it compares:**  
It shows something like “Current after grace: $X” and “New consolidation amount: $Y” so you can see whether consolidating would raise or lower your total monthly payment. Nothing in your real loans is changed—it’s for planning only.

# Detailed Payment Flow and Feature Linkage

This section explains how the app’s features connect. Think of it as “how one part affects another.”

---

## How loans affect Payment(now)

- **Private loans:** Each private loan has a current payment (based on balance, rate, and the active range). The app can add all of those together into **Payment(now)** when you click **Add current private loan payments to Payment(now)** or when you **recompute** (depending on setup).  
- **Public loans:** Your entered public loan payment is added into Payment(now) when you use **Add to Payment(now)** or **Use as current payment.**  
- So **Payment(now)** is the sum of whatever you’ve added from public and private. Editing Payment(now) overrides that number for display only.

---

## How Payment(now) feeds into recurring items

- In **Recurring**, you can create an expense whose amount is “current visible Payment(now) from Loans tab.”  
- That recurring item then **uses the same number** as the Payment(now) shown in Loans.  
- So: when you recompute or add loan payments in Loans, Payment(now) changes → the recurring “loan payment” expense updates to match. Your planned expenses stay in sync with your loan dashboard.

---

## Recurring → Upcoming → Pending

- **Recurring** items are templates: “I pay rent every month.”  
- You can **move** a recurring item (or a copy of it) into **Upcoming** when you want to plan a specific instance: “Rent due March 1.”  
- When you actually send the payment, you can **move** that upcoming item to **Pending outbound**. So the money is no longer “upcoming”—it’s “on its way out.” Your Snapshot then shows the payment as pending, which affects your final net cash.  
- When the transfer clears in real life, you can post or adjust in Snapshot so your balances match reality.  
So the flow is: Recurring (template) → Upcoming (planned) → Pending outbound (in motion) → then you update balances when it settles.

---

## Posting a loan payment

- When you **post** a loan payment in the app, you’re saying: “I made this payment; apply it to the loan.”  
- The app **updates the loan balance** (usually reduces it by the principal portion of the payment).  
- **Payment(now)** may change: for example, the public portion might be reduced or reset, and for private loans the **next time you recompute**, Payment(now) will use the new lower balances.  
- **Interest** continues to accrue on the remaining balance between recomputes; the next recompute will use the updated balance and rate.  
So: posting → balances go down → recompute → Payment(now) and payoff estimates reflect the new reality.

---

## Public and private both feed into Payment(now)

- **Payment(now)** can include **both** public and private loan payments.  
- You add the **public** part by using “Add to Payment(now)” or “Use as current payment” in the Public section.  
- You add the **private** part by using “Add current private loan payments to Payment(now)” or by **recomputing** (if the app sets Payment(now) from the recompute result).  
- So the single number Payment(now) is the combined “current loan payment” you see everywhere (Loans summary, recurring, etc.).

---

## Import/export and the rest of the app

- **Export JSON** saves a copy of **all** app data: accounts, balances, loans, spending, recurring, upcoming, settings, etc.  
- **Import JSON** **restores** that copy (usually replacing what’s currently in the app on that device).  
- So import/export is the way you back up and move your whole financial picture from one device to another or recover after clearing browser data. Nothing in the app is “outside” the backup—it’s one full snapshot.

---

## Appearance settings

- Theme, accent color, font family, and font size in **Settings** only change **how the app looks**.  
- They do **not** change any financial logic, balances, Payment(now), or calculations.  
- So you can customize the look without affecting your numbers.

## Appearance and financial logic

# Data, Privacy, and Storage

- The app **does not collect** your personal or financial information and send it to a backend server.  
- The app **does not store** your financial data in a shared cloud database.  
- **All data is stored locally** on the device and in the browser you use. Only you, on that device, see it.

**What that means:**

- **Different devices have separate data.** Your phone and your laptop each have their own local copy. They don’t sync unless you export on one and import on the other.  
- **Someone else opening the app on their own phone or laptop does not see your data.** They get their own empty (or their own) data. Your data never leaves your device unless you export it yourself.  
- **If you clear your browser’s storage** (cookies, site data, etc.), the app’s local data can be **lost**. So **exporting a JSON backup regularly** is recommended. If you clear storage and have no backup, you may lose everything you’ve entered.

For full details, see the in-app **Privacy** page (e.g. from Settings).

# Import / Export Backup Explanation

**Export JSON**  
- Creates a file on your device containing a **full backup** of your app data.  
- Use it to **back up** your data. Save the file somewhere safe (e.g. your computer or cloud storage).  
- **Exporting regularly is a good habit** so you always have a recent copy if something happens to the browser or device.

**Import JSON**  
- Lets you **restore** a previously exported backup. You choose the JSON file; the app loads it and **replaces** the current data on that device with the backup.  
- Use it when you switch devices, reinstall, or after clearing browser data.  
- **Importing restores that backup**; it doesn’t merge. So only import when you intend to replace what’s there.

**Why it matters:**  
Your data lives only in your browser and on your device. Backups are the only way to keep it safe and move it. Without export, clearing storage or losing the device can mean losing all your entries.

# Best Practices and Tips

- **Export a backup regularly** — For example after you’ve set up accounts, loans, and recurring items, and then on a schedule (e.g. monthly).  
- **Double-check variable rates before recomputing** — If you changed an interest rate, make sure it’s correct in the loan card before you recompute so Payment(now) and payoff estimates are accurate.  
- **Review Payment(now) before using it for recurring** — If you have a recurring expense that uses “current Payment(now),” check the Loans tab to see what that number is so you’re not surprised.  
- **Keep balances updated** — For the app to stay accurate, update bank and card balances when they change (e.g. after payments clear).  
- **Review spending categories** — Periodically check Settings and Spending to make sure categories match how you think about your money; adjust or add categories as needed.

# For Developers

If you want to run the app locally or deploy it:

1. **Install dependencies:** `npm install`  
2. **Start the dev server:** `npm run dev` — then open the URL shown (e.g. `http://localhost:5173/iisauhwallet/`) in your browser.  
3. **Optional backend:** The app works without a server. If you use the optional backend for extra features, see `server/README.md` for setup.  
4. **Deploy (e.g. GitHub Pages):** The repo can be set up to deploy on push to `main` (e.g. via GitHub Actions). Configure **Settings → Pages → Source: GitHub Actions** in the repo, then push to `main`; the built app is deployed from the `dist` output.

For privacy practices, see the in-app **Privacy** page.
